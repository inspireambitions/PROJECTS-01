## Part 1 — What Codie Sanchez and top-reach accounts actually do

Studied: **Codie Sanchez, Justin Welsh, Sahil Bloom, Matt Gray, Alex Hormozi, Dan Koe.** Different niches, same seven mechanics.

### 1. The hook is the product
Justin Welsh's data: his average hook is ~6 words vs. the typical 11, and he calls the first line "more important than 95% of the rest of the post." LinkedIn truncates at ~2 lines ("...see more"), so the first line has exactly one job: stop the scroll. Codie's signature move is **leading with what everyone gets wrong** — flip conventional wisdom before you present anything.

Hook archetypes they rotate:
- **Contrarian flip** (Codie): "Everyone tells you to buy startups. Buy boring businesses instead."
- **Relatable enemy** (Welsh): name something the audience already resents, take a stance against it.
- **Proof drop** (Hormozi/Gray): a specific number or result in line one.
- **Stakes question** (Bloom): "Why do prospects ghost after a great demo?"
- **Pattern break**: a sentence that contradicts the reader's expectation of the genre.

### 2. White space is a retention tactic, not a style choice
Welsh: ~5-word sentences, one-sentence paragraphs, air around every line. LinkedIn is read on phones; dense paragraphs die. But the 2026 caveat: **over-formatting (Unicode bold, emoji walls) now reads as AI-generated** and gets scrolled past. Clean short lines > decorated lines.

### 3. Format mix beats format loyalty (the big 2026 finding)
Current LinkedIn format data:
- **Document posts (PDF carousels): ~6.6% engagement — the highest of any format**, 2–4× the reach of text posts, driven by swipe-dwell time.
- **Text posts: ~2% engagement but the best comment-velocity format** — comments (especially from outside your network) are the strongest distribution signal, so text posts win burst reach in the first 24h.
- **Native video: reach per video post on personal profiles dropped ~36% YoY; ~0.86× reach multiplier.** Video still matters (vertical feed, face-to-brand trust) but it is no longer the reach engine.

The accounts that grow don't pick one format — they layer roughly **60% text / 40% carousel**, with video as a trust-and-depth layer, not the volume layer.

> **Implication for us:** do NOT build the LinkedIn strategy around posting the 36 HeyGen renders. The renders are one of three assets per script — and not the primary one.

### 4. The first 60 minutes decide distribution
The algorithm tests every post on a small sample; early engagement (dwell + comments) decides expansion. Top accounts engineer this: post at a consistent time, reply to every comment within the first hour, and spend 15 minutes commenting on adjacent large accounts immediately before and after posting.

### 5. Comments are the growth loop
Every top post ends with a **specific, low-effort question** — not "thoughts?" but "Which of the three would stretch you most?" Your script bank's CAPTION lines already do this correctly. Then the creator replies to every comment (each reply re-triggers distribution and doubles comment count).

### 6. Pillars + repetition, not novelty
Welsh, Gray, and Sanchez each rotate 3–5 content pillars and repost their winners every 6–8 weeks with a new hook. Nobody sees everything; repetition is a feature. Your bank already has clean pillars:
- Role explainers (9 scripts)
- Interview answers (10 scripts)
- Guest-service English "say this, not that" (10 scripts)
- Career growth / promotion evidence (5 scripts)
- Red flags & CV (2 scripts)

### 7. One CTA per post, and it's usually a comment prompt
Hard sells kill reach. The funnel is: post → comment prompt → profile → featured section / DM. Codie sells the *vision* ("own boring businesses, own your freedom") in content and the product only at the destination. For Inspire Ambitions the vision is: **"Gulf hospitality careers are built on evidence, not luck."**

## Part 2 — The repurposing system: 1 script = 3 LinkedIn assets

Your scripts are already structured like LinkedIn posts wearing a video costume. The mapping is almost mechanical:

| Script field | LinkedIn asset it becomes |
|---|---|
| HOOK (spoken) | Post first line (usually needs a sharper rewrite — see Part 3) |
| SCRIPT (avatar voice) | Text-post body, broken into white-space lines |
| ON-SCREEN TEXT (arrow beats) | Carousel slides — each `→` beat = one slide |
| CAPTION | Comment-prompt closer + video post copy |
| VERIFY | Compliance gate — applies to ALL formats, unchanged |

### Asset A — Text post (the volume engine, 3×/week)
Rewrite SCRIPT into Welsh-style formatting: hook line, one-sentence paragraphs, the model answer or phrase featured verbatim (it's copy-paste value — people save posts they can use tomorrow), close with the CAPTION question. 150–250 words. No link in the post body.

### Asset B — PDF carousel / document post (the reach engine, 2×/week)
The ON-SCREEN TEXT beats are a ready-made slide outline. Format: title slide = hook, one beat per slide with one supporting sentence pulled from SCRIPT, final slide = the comment prompt + "Follow Inspire Ambitions." 7–10 slides, 1080×1350, navy/white/gold brand palette (already defined in your HeyGen rules), large sans-serif. The "Stop saying X, say this instead" scripts are *born* for this format — save/share bait for every hotel worker in the Gulf.

### Asset C — Native vertical video (the trust engine, 1–2×/week)
The HeyGen render, posted natively (never a YouTube link). Requirements from 2026 video data: hook spoken in the first 1–2 seconds (your scripts already mandate "first spoken word at frame one" — good), burned-in captions (85% watch muted), 9:16 or 4:5, under 60–90 seconds. Post copy = CAPTION text with a rewritten first line. Expect lower reach than A and B; its job is face-to-brand trust and vertical-feed discovery, not volume.

**36 scripts × 3 assets = 108 posts ≈ 4–5 months of content at 5–6 posts/week, before any winner gets reposted.**

## Part 3 — Hook rewrites: HeyGen hooks vs. LinkedIn hooks

Your spoken hooks are good for video but often too long for a LinkedIn first line (truncation ≈ 60–70 visible characters). Rewrite rule: **cut to under ~8 words, front-load the tension, put the payoff after the fold.**

| Script | HeyGen hook | LinkedIn first line |
|---|---|---|
| 007 | "A hotel job offer that arrives with a payment request deserves five minutes of checking…" | **"A real job offer never asks you to pay."** |
| 024 | "When they ask about angry guests, they are not testing your patience…" | **"The angry-guest question isn't about patience."** |
| 039 | "In the history of hospitality, calm down has never once calmed anyone down." | Keep as-is — this one is already elite. |
| 048 | "The gap between front office agent and duty manager is not years. It is evidence." | **"Duty manager isn't a promotion. It's a verdict."** (or keep original — also strong) |
| 062 | "Nobody dreams of the laundry room. Plenty of supervisors started there." | Keep — perfect contrarian flip, very Codie. |
| 094 | "The five year question is not about your dreams. It is about their risk." | Keep — pattern-break already built in. |

Roughly a third of your hooks survive unchanged; the rest need the compression pass. Do it once per script during repurposing, A/B the two variants when reposting winners.

## Part 4 — Three worked examples

### Example 1 — Script 024 as a text post (Interview answers pillar)

```
The angry-guest question isn't about patience.

It's about boundaries.

Every Gulf hotel interviewer asks it.
Most candidates answer it wrong — they perform calmness.

Here's the answer structure that works (STAR):

Situation: A guest got the wrong room type after a long flight. Shouting. Full lobby.

Task: Resolve it without emptying the hotel's pockets.

Action: I listened without interrupting. Apologised for the experience.
Offered two options within my authority.
When he demanded a free week — I stayed polite and held the line.
Beyond policy. Escalated to my duty manager instead of promising it.

Result: He took the upgrade. Named me in a positive review.

The boundary is the point.

Hotels don't hire the most patient candidate.
They hire people who are kind and firm at the same time.

Practise yours aloud. Then tell me: which part is hardest for you?

(Model answer is a fictional example.)
```

## Part 5 — Engagement operating system (the part most accounts skip)

1. **Post at a fixed time** aligned to GCC mornings (07:30–08:30 GST — catches Gulf commutes and pre-shift phone time; hotel shift workers check phones early).
2. **15 minutes before posting:** leave 3–5 substantive comments on large adjacent accounts (hospitality leaders, GCC recruiters, hotel brands' hiring posts). Warms the algorithm and borrows their audience.
3. **First 60 minutes after posting:** reply to every comment. A reply is a new engagement event and doubles the comment count.
4. **Answer the CAPTION promises.** Several captions commit to replies ("Comment your role and I will suggest your proof line," "Comment your stage and I will tell you the proof project"). These are *retention gold* but only if honoured within hours — build a reply bank per script before posting.
5. **Repost winners** every 6–8 weeks with the alternate hook variant. Track: comments (primary), saves/sends (carousels), profile visits.
6. **Profile as landing page:** headline states the promise ("Helping you build a Gulf hospitality career on evidence, not luck"), Featured section holds the best carousel + the red-flags post.

## Part 6 — Compliance carry-over (non-negotiable)

The VERIFY gates transfer to every LinkedIn format, not just video:

- Every fictional scenario keeps its "fictional / composite" disclosure line in the post or final carousel slide.
- No employer names, salary figures, testimonials, or job guarantees in any format — including comment replies.
- Script 007 and 053's MEDIUM-HIGH gates (warning-signs framing, no blanket legal claims, no blanket rejection claims) apply verbatim to carousel slide text.
- Operational steps stay conditional ("where policy allows," "within your authority") in text and slides, exactly as in the scripts.
- Wrapper rows 102–399 stay quarantined: do not repurpose a wrapper until it has the role- or market-specific example its note requires. Repurpose masters only.
- Spot-check each carousel and text post against the master script before publishing — same rule as the render spot-check.
