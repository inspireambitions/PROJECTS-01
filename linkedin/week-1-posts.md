## Post 002 · Tuesday · Text — The angry-guest question (script 024)

The angry-guest question isn't about patience.

It's about boundaries.

Every Gulf hotel interviewer asks it.
Most candidates answer it wrong — they perform calmness.

Here's the structure that works (STAR):

Situation: A guest got the wrong room type after a long flight. Shouting. Full lobby.

Task: Resolve it without emptying the hotel's pockets.

Action: I listened without interrupting. Apologised for the experience.
Offered two options within my authority.
When he demanded a free week — I stayed polite and held the line.
Beyond policy. Escalated to my duty manager instead of promising it.

Result: He took the upgrade. Named me in a positive review.

The boundary is the point.

Hotels don't hire the most patient candidate.
They hire people who are kind and firm at the same time.

Practise yours aloud. Which part is hardest for you?

## Post 003 · Wednesday · Text — Stop saying "calm down" (script 039)

In the history of hospitality, "calm down" has never once calmed anyone down.

It tells an upset guest their feelings are the problem.
That doubles the anger.

De-escalate with validation and direction instead.

First, validate the concern — not the volume:

"I understand. This is not the stay you booked, and I want to fix it."

Notice what that does.
It agrees the problem matters, without agreeing to anything specific yet.

Then give two clear next steps:

"I can arrange a room change within the hour, or I can have the duty manager call you in ten minutes. Which works better for you?"

Choice returns control.
And control is what the guest actually lost.

One boundary: validation never extends to abuse.
If a guest becomes abusive — step back and escalate.
That's policy and self-respect.

What's the angriest situation you've had to handle?

## Post 005 · Friday · Text — "Tell me about yourself" in 30 seconds (script 004)

"Tell me about yourself" is not an invitation to tell your life story.

It's a thirty-second audition.

The formula: present, proof, reason.

A full model answer:

"I'm a guest service professional with three years in busy front-of-house roles.
In my last position I handled check-in for a two-hundred-room property and kept my queue moving during full occupancy.
I'm applying here because I want to grow in a five-star environment that takes service standards seriously."

That's it. Thirty seconds.

Now take the three blanks and make them yours:

→ Your role.
→ One result you can defend with detail.
→ One honest reason this hotel.

Practise it aloud five times before the interview.

Not memorised. Owned.

What role are you interviewing for? Comment and I'll suggest your proof line.
