Abu Dhabi Work Permits and Visas: The Process in Plain English

The Abu Dhabi work permit and visa process is mostly the same federal UAE process you would face in Dubai. But there are real Abu Dhabi-specific nuances worth knowing, especially the ADGM route, which runs under its own employment rules. This page lays out the numbers, the steps, and the points where your case might differ from a Dubai equivalent.

I am an HR Career Specialist, and I have walked candidates through both routes many times. Let me give you the structure first, then the details that matter.

Which route does your offer fall under?

Most private-sector roles in Abu Dhabi run under Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021, the same federal labour law that governs Dubai. Your employer applies for your work permit through the Ministry of Human Resources and Emiratisation, known as MOHRE, you enter on a two-month work permit, and your residence visa is stamped within 60 days. The full federal process sits on the UAE work visa process page, and almost all of it applies to Abu Dhabi private-sector roles unchanged.

Roles inside the Abu Dhabi Global Market, the ADGM, run under the ADGM's own employment regulations. The ADGM is a financial free zone with its own legal framework, modelled in spirit on English common law, with its own labour rules, courts, and dispute resolution. So if your offer is for an ADGM-based employer, the work permit and visa process is still administered through the federal system, but the employment relationship around it runs under different rules.

How the federal process works for Abu Dhabi roles

Step one is the offer and the work permit. Your employer secures a MOHRE work permit valid for two months, you receive your entry permit, and you travel to the UAE on that permit. Step two is the in-country completion, which includes your medical fitness test at an approved centre, your fingerprints, your Emirates ID application, and your labour card. Step three is the residence visa stamp, usually valid for two years on a standard private-sector job.

The whole post-arrival process must complete within 60 days of your entry. Delays almost always come from missing or unattested documents on the candidate side, not from slow government offices. So prepare your degree attestation, marriage certificate attestation if you bring family, and any other documents well in advance of the offer landing.

Who actually pays for the visa?

Under the same federal law that covers Dubai, your employer must bear the cost of your work permit and your visa. Anyone in Abu Dhabi telling you to pay tens of thousands of dirhams for your own work permit is either misinformed or trying to take advantage of you. The work permit fee itself sits between roughly AED 250 and AED 3,450, based on the employer's MOHRE classification, and that cost is the company's, not yours.

You do pay personal costs: your own passport, your own document attestation, your own medical insurance if it is not provided, and occasionally a small amount of administrative fees for personal services. The line is the same as I cover on the UAE cost and who pays page. Visa machinery is the employer's bill. Personal documents are yours.

What does the ADGM route mean for you?

If your offer is for an ADGM-based employer, the visa side runs through the federal system in much the same way. But your employment contract sits under the ADGM Employment Regulations, which differ from the federal law in several important ways. Notice periods, gratuity calculations, dispute resolution, and end-of-service procedures all run differently inside the ADGM.

I always tell candidates with ADGM offers to read their contract with fresh eyes. Do not assume Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021 will protect you in the same way. Many of the protections are similar, but the mechanics differ, and the differences can matter at exit. I once helped a senior banker with an ADGM contract review the gratuity clause carefully before signing. Bringing your family

Once your own residence visa is issued, you can usually sponsor your spouse and children as dependants on your visa, provided your salary meets the threshold. The threshold shifts over time and varies slightly by case, but the principle is steady. Your own status comes first. The family files build on it.

I always advise candidates not to rush both at once. A few weeks of overlap, where the worker arrives first and the family follows once schools and housing are arranged, almost always reduces the stress on everyone. The relocation side is covered on the relocating here page, including schools, housing, and the realistic timeline for a family move.

Renewing and changing employers

Your residence visa is valid for two years on a standard private-sector role, and renewing it is a lighter version of the original process. Your employer files the renewal, you repeat the medical test, you update your Emirates ID, and you confirm your health insurance is live. Renewal works the same way for ADGM roles, though the wider employment relationship around it follows the ADGM rules.

If you change employers, the federal labour reforms of recent years make moving far easier than the older horror stories suggested. I cover the full picture on the cancellation and transfer page. The same rules apply to Abu Dhabi private-sector roles. ADGM-to-federal and federal-to-ADGM moves carry a few extra mechanics worth checking with your new employer's HR before you sign.

The checks every candidate should run

Before you sign anything, run three checks. First, confirm the employer is real and registered, and ask which regulatory framework your role sits under. Second, ask the employer to confirm that the work permit and visa costs are theirs, not yours, in writing. Third, ensure your contract is properly attested and matches what you were promised verbally. The Abu Dhabi market has its own attestation rhythm, and a clean contract early avoids tangles later.

The capital rewards the careful candidate. Treat the visa as a serious project from the day you sign, not a formality to wave through. Get those few weeks right and your whole working life in Abu Dhabi rests on firm ground.

Common questions about Abu Dhabi work permits and visas

Is the Abu Dhabi work permit the same as the Dubai one?
For private-sector roles, yes. Both run under Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021, with the work permit issued by MOHRE and a two-year residence visa. Roles inside the ADGM follow the federal visa process but the ADGM's own employment regulations.

Who pays for an Abu Dhabi work visa?
Your employer. Under the same federal labour law that covers Dubai, the company must bear the cost of your work permit and your visa. The fee runs from around AED 250 to AED 3,450, based on the employer's MOHRE classification.

What is the ADGM and how does it affect my visa?
The Abu Dhabi Global Market is a financial free zone with its own employment regulations, modelled in spirit on English common law. The visa process still runs through the federal system, but the employment relationship around it follows ADGM rules, which differ in notice, gratuity, and dispute mechanics.
